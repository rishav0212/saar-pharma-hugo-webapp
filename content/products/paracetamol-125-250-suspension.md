## Description & Therapeutic Class {#overview}
Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) is a centrally acting, non-opioid analgesic and antipyretic. Unlike NSAIDs, it exerts minimal anti-inflammatory action, which translates to a vastly superior safety profile—specifically, the complete absence of gastrointestinal bleeding risks.

## Clinical Pharmacology & Batch Integrity {#mechanism}
**Mechanism of Action:**
Paracetamol primarily acts within the central nervous system (CNS). It reduces fever through direct action on the hypothalamic heat-regulating center, inducing vasodilation and sweating to dissipate body heat. Its analgesic properties are achieved by elevating the pain threshold, likely through the inhibition of COX enzymes (specifically COX-2 and COX-3) within the brain, without inhibiting peripheral prostaglandins.

## Safety Profile & Contraindications {#safety}
Paracetamol is exceptionally safe when administered within the recommended therapeutic window. However, B2B partners must ensure clear labeling regarding contraindications:
- **Hepatotoxicity Risk**: The primary risk is liver damage caused by acute overdosage (exceeding 60 mg/kg/day in children). It is contraindicated in patients with severe hepatic impairment.
- **Adverse Reactions**: Allergic skin rashes or mild gastrointestinal discomfort occur very rarely. 
- **Drug Interactions**: Caution is required when co-administered with drugs that induce hepatic microsomal enzymes (e.g., phenobarbital, carbamazepine), as this can increase the risk of liver toxicity.
